## DAY 56 - A2.1.56: Relative Pronouns (qui/que) - Basics

### Metadata
- **CEFR Level:** A2.1
- **Grammar Topic:** Relative pronouns qui (subject) and que (object)
- **Vocabulary Domain:** People, objects, descriptions
- **Content Identifiers:** relative_pronouns, qui, que, complex_sentences
- **Speaking Tier:** 2 (Guided conversation)
- **Duration:** 30 minutes
- **Day Number:** 56

---

### GRAMMAR SECTION (10 min)

#### Explanation

**1. Why Use Relative Pronouns?**

Relative pronouns help you **connect two sentences** so you can describe people and things without repeating nouns. Instead of: "C'est un ami. Il habite à Lyon," you can say: "C'est un ami **qui** habite à Lyon." This is more natural and fluent.

**2. QUI = Subject of the Verb**

Use **qui** when the person or thing **does the action** in the relative clause.
- C'est la femme **qui** parle. (She is speaking.)
- Voici le film **qui** commence à 20h. (The film starts at 8.)

**3. QUE = Object of the Verb**

Use **que** when the person or thing **receives the action**.
- C'est le livre **que** j'aime. (I like the book.)
- Voici la photo **que** tu as prise. (You took the photo.)

**4. Quick Test**

Replace the noun with **il/elle**:
- If it becomes **il/elle** (subject) → use **qui**
- If it becomes **le/la** (object) → use **que**

**5. Real-World Use**

Relative pronouns are common in introductions, recommendations, and descriptions. You will use them in travel stories, product choices, and everyday conversations.

**Cultural Note:**
In French, relative clauses are used frequently in polite descriptions: "C'est un restaurant qui est très calme" sounds smoother than repeating the noun.

## DAY 57 - A2.1.57: Relative Pronoun "où" - Place and Time

### Metadata
- **CEFR Level:** A2.1
- **Grammar Topic:** Relative pronoun où (place/time)
- **Vocabulary Domain:** Places, time references
- **Content Identifiers:** relative_pronouns, ou, place, time
- **Speaking Tier:** 2 (Guided conversation)
- **Duration:** 30 minutes
- **Day Number:** 57

---

### GRAMMAR SECTION (10 min)

#### Explanation

**1. What Is "où"?**

**Où** replaces a place or time expression. It means **where** or **when** in a relative clause.
- C'est la ville **où** je suis né. (That is the city **where** I was born.)
- C'est le jour **où** nous sommes partis. (That is the day **when** we left.)

**2. Où for Places**

Use **où** after a place word: ville, endroit, restaurant, école, bureau.
- Voici l'endroit **où** je travaille. (Here is the place where I work.)

**3. Où for Time**

Use **où** after time nouns: jour, moment, année, époque.
- C'était l'année **où** nous avons voyagé. (It was the year when we traveled.)

**4. Common Patterns**

- le lieu où...
- le moment où...
- la journée où...

**5. Real-World Use**

You will use "où" in travel stories, personal memories, and descriptions of places.

**Cultural Note:**
French speakers often use "où" to connect memories: "l'année où j'ai appris le français" is a very common phrase.

## DAY 58 - A2.1.58: Relative Pronouns Review + Complex Sentences

### Metadata
- **CEFR Level:** A2.1
- **Grammar Topic:** Review of qui, que, où + sentence building
- **Vocabulary Domain:** Routines, events, daily life
- **Content Identifiers:** relative_pronouns, review, complex_sentences
- **Speaking Tier:** 2→3 (Guided to freer conversation)
- **Duration:** 30 minutes
- **Day Number:** 58

---

### GRAMMAR SECTION (10 min)

#### Explanation

**1. Review the Three Tools**

- **qui** = subject (person/thing does the action)
- **que** = object (person/thing receives the action)
- **où** = place or time

**2. Combine with Two Clauses**

Use these to create **longer, more fluent sentences**:
- C'est le café **où** nous allons.
- Voici le film **que** tu aimes.
- C'est la personne **qui** parle.

**3. Order and Punctuation**

Relative clauses go **right after the noun** they describe. Keep sentences clear by placing the relative clause immediately after the noun.

**4. Mixing Qui/Que/Où**

You can use more than one in a paragraph:
- C'est la ville **où** j'ai grandi et c'est le professeur **qui** m'a aidé.

**5. Communication Tip**

Using relative clauses makes your French sound more natural and connected. It is a key step toward A2 and B1 fluency.

**Cultural Note:**
In French, descriptive sentences are often linked with relative clauses to avoid repetition.

### GRAMMAR SECTION (10 min)

#### Explanation

**1. Review the Month 3 Toolkit**

- Imparfait formation and usage
- Passé composé vs. imparfait (events vs. background)
- Comparatives and superlatives
- Relative pronouns (qui/que/où)

**2. How to Choose the Tense**

- **Imparfait:** habits, descriptions, background
- **Passé composé:** completed events

**3. How to Compare**

- **plus/moins/aussi + adjective + que**
- **plus/moins/autant de + noun**
- **verb + plus/moins/autant**
- **le/la/les plus/moins + adjective**

**4. How to Connect Sentences**

- **qui** = subject
- **que** = object
- **où** = place/time

**5. Success Tip**
